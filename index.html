Brier Score

A single number that measures how accurate your probability predictions are. Lower is better. It's the standard scoring method for binary forecasting — the prediction market equivalent of MAPE.

The Formula

For each resolved trade: (predicted probability − actual outcome)²

Outcome is 1 if the event happened (YES resolved), 0 if it didn't (NO resolved). Average across all resolved trades for your overall score.

Score Benchmarks

SCOREWHAT IT MEANSCONTEXT
< 0.10EliteSuperforecasters on Metaculus with thousands of trades
0.10 – 0.15Very goodReal edge, solid calibration — aspire to be here
0.15 – 0.20DecentBetter than most, room to sharpen — realistic target
0.20 – 0.25Coin-flip territoryNot adding much over random guessing
> 0.25Worse than randomActively miscalibrated

Why Squaring Matters

The squaring is intentional — it punishes confident wrong predictions disproportionately. Saying 90% on something that doesn't happen scores (0.90 − 0)² = 0.81. Saying 60% on the same outcome scores only 0.36. This creates an incentive to only go high-conviction when you actually have edge.

Being confidently wrong is far more damaging than being uncertain and wrong. Never inflate your probability to match the market price — log what you actually believe.

How to Improve It

Fix overconfidence in high buckets. Check the calibration page. If your 80–90% bucket is underperforming, shade your stated probability down or stop betting there.

Separate Sports vs Current Events. Most people are better calibrated in one category. Lean into your stronger one.

Watch the L20 rolling score. The overall average is dragged by old trades. The last-20 on the dashboard shows current form.

Target: under 0.20 is good. Under 0.15 is the aspirational goal for Kalshi-style binary markets.

Calibration

Calibration measures whether your predicted probabilities match your actual win rates across many trades. If you say 70% on 20 different trades, you should win roughly 14 of them. If you win 18, you're underconfident. If you win 10, you're overconfident.

How the Calibration Chart Works

Trades are grouped into 10-percentage-point buckets based on your effective probability — the probability that your direction wins. A YES bet at 75% has an effective probability of 75%. A NO bet at 30% has an effective probability of 70%.

The dashed line is perfect calibration. Your green line is actual performance. Points above the dashed line mean you're winning more than expected — potential edge. Points below mean you're getting burned.

Edge Labels

LABELCONDITIONWHAT TO DO
Strong EdgeWinning 20%+ more than expectedBet more here — market underpricing you
Possible EdgeWinning 10–20% more than expectedWatch and accumulate more data
EfficientWithin 10% of expectedMarket is fairly priced here
Slight BleedLosing 10–20% more than expectedMonitor — could be variance
Getting BurnedLosing 20%+ more than expectedAvoid or fade this bucket
Early exits are excluded from calibration. They count toward P&L but not toward your probability accuracy — since the outcome was never resolved, there's nothing to score against.

Sample Size Warning

A bucket with fewer than 10 trades is statistically unreliable. A 3-trade bucket showing "Strong Edge" could easily be noise. Use calibration labels directionally, and weight them more heavily as trade counts grow.

Sharpe Ratio

Named after economist William Sharpe, this measures risk-adjusted return — how much profit you're making relative to how volatile your results are. A high Sharpe means consistent profits. A low Sharpe means your results are noisy, even if the average is positive.

The Formula

(average return − risk-free rate) / standard deviation of returns

In a prediction market context, the risk-free rate is typically set to 0, so it simplifies to mean return divided by the volatility of your returns across trades.

Benchmarks

SHARPEINTERPRETATION
< 0Losing money on average
0 – 0.5Weak — wins are masking losses
0.5 – 1.0Acceptable but inconsistent
1.0 – 2.0Good — consistent edge
> 2.0Strong — repeatable, low-noise edge

Why It Matters for Kalshi

Your P&L total can look positive while hiding the fact that you had one big lucky win and many small losses. Sharpe exposes this. Two traders with identical total P&L can have very different Sharpe ratios — the one with consistent, lower-variance wins is the better forecaster.

ma.estro doesn't currently calculate Sharpe automatically, but you can track it in your Sheet using the actual_pnl column. Sharpe is most meaningful after 50+ resolved trades.

Closing Line Value (CLV)

CLV measures whether the market moved toward or away from your position after you placed your bet. It's widely considered the single best indicator of long-term forecasting edge — better than win rate alone.

How It Works

If you buy YES at 60% and the market closes at 75%, you have positive CLV — the market came around to your view. If it closes at 45%, you had negative CLV — the market moved against you even if you happened to win.

CLV = closing probability − your entry probability (for YES bets, flip sign for NO bets)

Why CLV Beats Win Rate

Win rate is noisy in the short term — you can be right for the wrong reasons. CLV strips out luck. If you consistently buy before the market moves your way, you have genuine information edge. If your CLV is negative but your win rate is positive, you're running hot and it likely won't last.

To track CLV, you need to log the market's closing price at resolution — not just your entry. Consider adding a closing_prob column to your Sheet and tracking this manually for a few months before building automation around it.

CLV and Kalshi

Kalshi markets are relatively thin compared to sports books, which means CLV signals are noisier. A single large trader can move a market. Use CLV directionally, not as a precise number, especially in lower-volume markets.

Bucket Analysis

Bucket analysis groups your trades by predicted probability range and asks: within each range, are you winning at the rate you expected? It's how you find where your edge actually lives.

Reading the Drill-Down

Click any bucket card on the calibration page to see every individual trade in that range. Look for patterns: are your losses clustered in certain market types? Certain time periods? Certain categories?

Acting on Buckets

SITUATIONACTION
Strong edge bucket, low trade countActively look for more markets in this range
Getting burned bucket, high trade countStop betting here until you understand why
Efficient bucketFine to trade here but don't expect to beat the market
Any bucket under 8 tradesTreat as inconclusive — variance is too high

The Effective Probability Adjustment

ma.estro calculates your effective probability — the probability that your direction wins — rather than just your raw entry probability. A NO bet at 30% has an effective probability of 70%. This matters for bucketing because a NO at 30% is behaviorally similar to a YES at 70%: you're betting the high-confidence side either way.

Bucket analysis is most powerful after 100+ resolved trades. Before that, use it directionally. After that, it becomes a genuine decision-making tool for market selection.

Position Sizing

How much you bet matters as much as whether you're right. Flat staking (always betting the same amount) is simple but leaves edge on the table. Kelly Criterion is the mathematically optimal approach but requires accurate probability estimates.

Flat Staking

Bet the same dollar amount on every trade. Simple, easy to track, protects against ruin. The downside: you're not pressing your edge when you have it. Good starting point while building your calibration dataset.

Kelly Criterion

Kelly tells you what fraction of your bankroll to bet given your edge: f = (bp − q) / b where b is the decimal odds, p is your probability of winning, and q is 1 − p.

Full Kelly is aggressive and most practitioners use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly to reduce variance. The formula only works as well as your probability estimates — a miscalibrated input produces a bad bet size.

Don't use Kelly sizing until you have at least 50 resolved trades and a Brier Score under 0.20. Before that, your probability estimates aren't reliable enough to drive sizing decisions.

Practical Approach

A simple middle ground: use two or three stake tiers. Normal conviction gets your base stake. High conviction (buckets where you have demonstrated edge) gets 1.5x. Low conviction or unfamiliar market types get 0.5x. Track which tier performs best over time.

Early Exits

An early exit means you closed your position before the market resolved — taking profit or cutting a loss rather than waiting for the outcome. Early exits count toward your P&L but are excluded from calibration and Brier Score.

Why They're Excluded from Calibration

Calibration measures whether your probability predictions are accurate. Since early exits don't have a final outcome, there's nothing to score your prediction against. Including them would introduce noise into your accuracy metrics.

When to Exit Early

SCENARIOCONSIDERATION
Market moved your way sharplyLock in profit if you're near max value. Staying in for the last few cents often isn't worth the binary risk.
New information changes your viewExit immediately. Don't let sunk-cost thinking keep you in a trade your updated model says to leave.
Position is underwaterExit if your original thesis is broken. Hold if only the price moved but your reasoning still holds.
Liquidity is thin near resolutionThin markets can gap. Exiting early at a fair price beats getting stuck.
Track your early exit P&L separately from resolved P&L. If your exits are consistently profitable, that's a skill. If they're consistently locking in losses you could have recovered, that's a pattern to fix.
